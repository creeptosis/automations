# Load management & injury prevention

Sources:
- Brant Stachel (former Canadian national team coach, registered
  psychotherapist, coached Olympians and 150+ Boston qualifiers; transcripts
  garble his name as "Brad Stulberg"/"Brent Statchel"):
  - "The Mileage Amount That Actually Gets Runners Injured", youtube.com/watch/ZuEQ61pLP0g (added 2026-07-02)
  - Baseline mileage vs the 10% rule, YouTube transcript (added 2026-07-02)

## Core principle
Injury risk is driven by the **gap between what the body has consistently
handled and what you suddenly ask it to do** — not by absolute weekly mileage.
(2014 JOSPT study: rate of change relative to adapted load predicted injury;
total mileage did not.) Not all miles are equal: a mile at 70% of your
baseline is a completely different physiological event than a mile at 120%.

The classic 10% week-over-week rule fails in both directions: below your
adapted level it's a rounding-error stimulus (a wasted training week), and
above it it keeps stacking stress onto a system already at its ceiling. This
is why runners who follow it religiously still get injured while others who
"violate" it stay healthy.

## Baseline mileage — the planning anchor
The highest weekly volume you can sustain week after week, near-indefinitely,
without accumulating meaningful fatigue, soreness, or mental burden. It is a
**band, not a number** (e.g. 35-45 mi/wk); within the band you move freely.
It is NOT peak mileage and NOT goal mileage. Prefer prescribing in **minutes
per week** — the body knows time, not distance.

Find it from the last 6-12 months of training data with three questions:
1. At what volume do I feel strong and consistent — no dread, no permanently
   heavy legs?
2. At what point does volume start costing me — beat up, motivation drops,
   niggles appear?
3. Where does the mental weight of training become a burden?

Baseline = the upper end of the range where Q1 is yes and Q2/Q3 are no.
Typical: recreational 30-50 mi/wk (~50-80 km), competitive 50-70 mi/wk.

**Baseline is the midpoint of a season's mileage arc, not the ceiling.** E.g.
a 45 mi/wk-baseline marathoner on a 20-week cycle: start ~35, pass baseline
around week 8, peak 55-60 in weeks 14-16, taper.

## Progression rules
- **Below baseline (rebuilding):** build back aggressively — 20-30% weekly
  jumps are fine (e.g. 15 -> 25 -> 30). The body is returning to known
  territory, not adapting to new load. Exception: after a break longer than
  ~10 days, still treat the first 2 weeks as a rebuilding block (comeback
  trap, below).
- **At/above baseline (new territory):** 5-10% increases every 2-3 weeks,
  NOT every week, with **flat consolidation weeks** between (e.g. baseline 40
  building to 50: 42, 42, 45, 45, 48). Flat weeks are where connective tissue
  and bone catch up to cardiovascular fitness — remove them and accumulation
  outpaces consolidation, which is the injury mechanism.
- Reference for "consistently handled" = the **3-week rolling average**, not
  last week or your best week.
- **No single run >10% longer than the longest run of the past 3 weeks.**
- Add easy volume in ~5-minute increments on easy runs, not in jumps.
- **Never add volume and intensity in the same week.** Intensity causes more
  injuries than volume (see [intensity-discipline.md](intensity-discipline.md)).
- Before any session ask: what is the gap between what my body has
  consistently handled and what I'm about to ask it to do?

## Three danger zones
1. **Comeback trap** — after a break, cardio fitness returns fast but tendons,
   ligaments and fascia decondition *and* recondition slower. You feel fine,
   jump back to old volume, get away with it for ~7-14 days, then break.
   Rule: any break longer than 10 days -> first 2 weeks are a rebuilding
   block regardless of how good you feel.
2. **Race schedule squeeze** — counting backwards from race day and forcing a
   faster-than-safe progression to hit peak mileage in time. Breakdown
   typically hits at week 6-10, right at peak stress. Rule: if the math from
   your current baseline at safe build rates doesn't reach the required
   volume within the runway, either extend the runway, lower the peak-volume
   target, or pick a different race — "better 100% healthy and 90% fit than
   100% fit and 0% healthy."
3. **Feel-good trap** — injuries are a *lagging* indicator of training stress;
   feeling great is not the same as being ready for more. Your best training
   weeks are your highest-risk weeks; that's when discipline matters most.

## Consistency is the mechanism
Holding 40 mi/wk for 52 straight weeks accumulates more adaptive stimulus
than spiking to 65, breaking at week 10, and rebuilding for 6. Most plateaued
runners are under-planned, not under-talented: the build-injure-rebuild cycle
resets fitness lower each time, so years of hard work never compound.
Corollary: test volume tolerance in the off-season (with B/C races on the
calendar) so the goal-race build uses loads already proven to work.

## Early warning signs (act, don't push)
- Soreness that doesn't clear within 48h. Uniform/bilateral soreness after a
  big session is tolerable; **localized soreness on one side is a red flag**.
- The first minutes of runs feel unusually hard — on easy AND hard days.
- Unexplained loss of motivation — a physiological protection signal, not a
  mental-toughness failure.

## Response when warning signs appear
Immediate mandatory easy week: drop intensity, swap some runs for low-impact
cross-training (swim/bike/elliptical). Caught early this costs 3-7 days;
pushed through, it becomes a 5-7 week layoff. Adaptation is measured in months
to years, not workouts and weeks — consistency beats any single session.
