# Tempo / threshold training

Sources:
- Brant Stachel, tempo & threshold explainer, youtube.com/watch/yaxGoBqpRPE (added 2026-07-02)

## What tempo is
A **physiological target, not a watch pace**: work near LT2 (anaerobic
threshold), where aerobic metabolism still leads but lactate starts rising.
Pace, heart rate and lactate are all *proxies* for that stimulus. Practical
definition: **the fastest pace you could hold ~45-60 minutes in a race,
fresh and evenly paced** (10k-half marathon race duration for most amateurs).
Purpose: push LT2 forward — raise the speed you can sustain before lactate
overwhelms you. One of the strongest performance predictors.

Terminology:
- **LT1** (aerobic threshold): top of easy/endurance, lactate ~1.0-2.5 mmol,
  RPE ~4/10, end of zone 2.
- **LT2** (anaerobic threshold): what people actually mean by
  "tempo"/"threshold". Lactate ~4 mmol (Daniels T) or 2-3 mmol (Norwegian),
  RPE ~7/10, HR ~82-88% of max, late zone 4.
- Marathon pace is NOT tempo — for most amateurs MP is dramatically slower
  (a 2-4h effort vs a ~1h effort). They only converge near elite level
  (half marathon ~75 min or faster).

## Daniels vs Norwegian
- **Daniels T-pace**: derived from a recent race performance via VDOT tables
  (~4 mmol). Bread-and-butter sessions: 20-30 min continuous, or cruise
  intervals (e.g. 3x10 min with short jog). Keep T volume ~10% of weekly
  mileage (up to ~15% in specific phases).
- **Norwegian lactate-controlled**: prescribes to internal physiology,
  lactate held at 2-3 mmol — but the real method requires a lactate meter
  and testing after *every rep*. A single lab test only tells you where LT2
  was on that day on that treadmill. **Not applicable for most non-elites**;
  don't run "Norwegian" off one lab report. For self-coached runners, the
  Daniels approach wins.

## Finding tempo without a lab — triangulate
1. Recent race -> VDOT -> T-pace (adjust for heat/altitude/terrain).
2. Breathing/feel: "comfortably hard" — short sentences possible, not gasping.
3. HR ~82-88% max as a sanity check (watch-derived max HR is often invalid).
4. The key question, asked early and often during the session: **"could I
   hold this effort for 45-60 minutes?"** If no — you're above LT2, slow
   down. (Don't answer it in the first 2 minutes; anything feels holdable
   for 2 minutes.)

## Execution rules & common mistakes
- **Better under the curve and slower than over it and faster.** Going over
  LT2 turns tempo into a VO2max session and loses the workout's purpose.
  Fading by minute 10-12 = you went out too fast.
- Ease in: run the first 1-2 km a few seconds slow, especially on tired legs.
- Sessions must be long enough to matter: ~20 min continuous or 25-30 min
  total broken with SHORT recoveries.
- **Cruise-interval recoveries are short easy jogs (~1 min on 5x5 min)** —
  not standing/phone breaks. If you need long recoveries, you're running the
  reps too fast. Long recovery = lactate drops = you never accumulate time
  near LT2.
- Adjust to conditions instead of serving the watch: heat/humidity can
  legitimately cost 30 s/km; hills, wind, altitude, and surface all shift
  pace at equal effort. Bombing sessions by refusing to adjust costs ~2 days
  each time and compounds to ~1.5 lost training weeks per 12-week build.
- Tempo doesn't replace the rest: you still need easy volume, VO2max work,
  and reps. No single-zone philosophy (see
  [intensity-discipline.md](intensity-discipline.md)).

## Session templates by focus
- **10k focus**: build to 20-25 min continuous tempo, or 3x10 min with 1-2
  min jog, once per week. **Add minutes before adding speed.**
- **Half marathon focus**: build over 6-8 weeks to 2x20 min with 4 min jog,
  peaking ~12 days out. Weekly shape: Tue tempo, Fri VO2max, Sun long run.
  Hitting 2x20 at tempo is a good half-marathon-pace predictor.
- **Marathon focus**: same toolbox (4x10, 3x8, 2x20). Marathon success =
  total aerobic volume + marathon-pace work + tempo work.
