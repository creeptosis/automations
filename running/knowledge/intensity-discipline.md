# Intensity discipline (easy/hard separation)

Sources:
- Unattributed text dump from Ben (added 2026-07-02)
- YouTube comment exchange between Ben (@BennJQ) and Brant Stachel re
  double threshold & weekly structure (added 2026-07-02)

## Core principle
Runners get injured from lack of intensity discipline, not lack of effort.
Improvement comes from properly separating easy and hard running — not from
working harder every day. Elites are better because they train with precision:
easy days stay easy, hard days are executed well, recovery windows are treated
as seriously as the workouts.

## Rules
- **Easy runs genuinely easy**: full-conversation pace, and you should be
  recovered before the next session.
- Moderate-grind "easy" runs are the worst of both worlds: fatigue without
  workout-level benefit, plus degraded quality on the hard days.
- **Max ~2 true quality sessions per week** for most runners; everything else
  exists to support recovery and aerobic development.
- **At least 48h between hard sessions, preferably ~72h.**
- **Stop a workout when quality breaks down** — no grinding out junk reps.
- Protect hard days: keep them specific and spaced; don't let them drift into
  extra volume or extra intensity.

## Why spacing matters
The cardiovascular system feels ready before tendons, ligaments and fascia
have recovered — stacking intensity too close together is a prime source of
overuse injury. Same connective-tissue lag that drives the comeback trap in
[load-management-and-injury-prevention.md](load-management-and-injury-prevention.md).

## Brant's "normal" weekly template
For a 5k/10k-focused runner (he's used it from 13:38 to 30:00 10k level —
percentage-based paces scale across levels):

    Mon E / Tue Tempo / Wed E / Thu E / Fri Intervals / Sat off-or-E / Sun LR

Two quality days (Tue/Fri, 72h apart) + long run. **No doubles unless a
single day exceeds ~70-75 min of running** (or life logistics force a split).

## Double threshold — mostly not for amateurs
- For most non-elites there is "far more to be gained from normal training
  than doing double-T". Amateurs struggle to hit double-T ranges because of
  **peripheral durability (tendons, muscles, recovery cost), not
  cardiovascular fitness** — elites holding threshold at a higher %MHR is
  heavily tied to peripheral durability, not just engine size.
- HR guides: threshold ≈ 82-92% MHR; staying **<88% MHR is the safe zone**
  for sub-threshold volume work (double-T PM sessions target <3.5 mmol).
- Threshold %MHR is NOT constant across levels: beginners sit lower
  (~80-85% MHR), well-trained higher (~88-92%) — it's trainable.
- Practical bias: err slightly lower intensity + more volume on T sessions.
